# BVLOS Operations — Lessons Learnt (16 June – 11 July 2026)

## Pre-Flight Planning & Risk Assessment
- Waypoint (WP) missions do **not** follow terrain — the aircraft flies a direct line between one waypoint's altitude and the next. Insert intermediate "height-control" waypoints over high ground or obstacles so the climb/descent gradient always stays clear of the terrain slope; without them, a shallow climb or descent between two distant waypoints can fly the aircraft into rising ground.
- Be clear on the distinction between a waypoint's set altitude (AGL, relative to that point) and the home-referenced ("green") altitude shown on the display — confusing the two puts the aircraft at the wrong height.
- Know the difference between a direct ascent after takeoff and a safe takeoff profile before building a WP mission — assumptions here propagate into every leg.
- For large-area mapping, dissect the area in Google Earth Pro first: import the client KML, draw smaller polygons slightly *outside* the given boundary (so edges are fully covered), and size each so the drone travels roughly 15–20 km per battery cycle (closer to the home dock, nearer 20 km is fine). Overlap adjacent sub-areas by ~30–50 m so tiles stitch, then export to FH2 to confirm flight times and adjust as required.
- For large or recurring WP missions, build and save a **reversed route in advance**. FH2 cannot resume a mission after a user-triggered or disconnection-triggered smart RTH, so if the link drops mid-mission a prepared reverse lets you continue from the far end instead of re-flying already-captured area to reach the break point.
- Read the local departure/arrival procedures (DAPs) thoroughly before operating near an aerodrome — understanding the runway-in-use logic for the day's wind is what lets you judge actual conflict rather than applying a blanket buffer.
- Expect the electronic flight bag / position app (e.g. OzRunways) to need an internet connection — plan for the possibility that a data dropout removes your ADSB/position picture mid-operation.
- Know your GSD error tolerance (roughly 2–3× the target GSD, in mm) before flying, so an in-flight decision to relax GSD for altitude/connection reasons is an informed one.

## Airmanship & Flight Technique
- When a WP leg exists only to control height (not to trigger a photo/action), enabling flight-follow makes the aircraft continuously track toward the *next* waypoint's altitude — a direct ascent/descent. Use this deliberately to shape the vertical profile: climb to a WP set at the obstacle height, then descend to the following one.
- Flying close to an operational boundary or through a tight corridor can significantly cut transit speed (observed ~15 m/s dropping to ~10 m/s) — a large penalty when the target area is several km from the hub. Factor the slower transit into battery and timing calculations.
- Don't forget the aircraft can capture stills and video **directly** on command. For a quick "grab a few images here and there" task, shoot directly rather than falling back on screenshots of the RGB/thermal live view.
- On poor-solar/rainy days, conserve dock and system power by switching unused docks off.
- Thermal has situational limits: for leakage detection, warm saline (~30–35 °C) shows a golden signature in thermal, but an under-trained model can over-call anomalies (early runs flagged roughly half the site). Until the thermal model matures, weight RGB more heavily — thermal's real value is on wet/overnight-rain days, to distinguish genuine leakage from rain ponding.

## Airspace Coordination & Traffic Deconfliction
- "No arrival/departure-path conflict within ~10 minutes of aircraft time" is hard to apply in practice — aircraft take off early or arrive late far more often than they run exactly on time. Treat time-based buffers as a starting point, not a guarantee.
- Assess conflict against the **actual runway in use for the day's wind**. Example: a drone west of the runway has no conflict when arrivals use one direction, but the reciprocal wind puts arriving traffic overhead with possibly insufficient vertical separation. The pilot must actively assess, not assume.
- A job-card instruction to "stay off the departure path" can still leave tight separation — aircraft turn during climb, so being laterally clear of the nominal departure track doesn't guarantee vertical clearance when they pass overhead. Watch the climb turn, not just the runway centreline.
- A high authorised operating ceiling (e.g. ~3,100 ft) can *increase* the risk of vertical conflict with traffic climbing through similar levels — treat a high ceiling as something to justify, not headroom to fill by default.
- Manned traffic conducting linear infrastructure work (e.g. powerline stringing and pole-by-pole platform operations) moves at variable, sometimes unexpected times and speeds between phases — don't assume it's on the ground just because it isn't audible; keep tracking it.
- A position/ADSB picture that depends on connectivity is a single point of failure — cross-check with radio and other situational-awareness sources so a data dropout doesn't leave you blind.

## Communications (CRM)
- Departure calls may only reach you once the aircraft is airborne. A ~5-minute delayed departure was first heard only on the airborne radio call, compressing reaction time — a quiet frequency is **not** confirmation that scheduled traffic hasn't launched.
- When altitudes are exchanged with manned aircraft, keep unit conventions explicit (drone displays metres AGL; aviation calls use feet) — carry the deliberate-conversion habit from the previous period into every concurrent operation.

## In-Flight Decision-Making
- If a mission fails at a specific, repeatable point (e.g. a connection dropout in a pit), **adjust the flight plan before re-attempting**. Re-running the identical mission and hoping the link holds tends to fail the same way; changing altitude/GSD or routing is what actually resolves it.
- Set the mission start point closest to the known worst spot (e.g. the deepest, most shielded part of a pit). If the link drops there you can reload and retry quickly, rather than discovering the dropout only near the end and having to re-fly the whole area.
- Remember FH2 cannot resume after a user-triggered RTH or a disconnection smart-RTH — a dropout late in a long mission means re-flying from the start unless a reversed route was prepared.
- Reversing a flight plan to skip completed waypoints does **not** carry the per-waypoint actions (gimbal angle, camera settings). The aircraft flies the reversed geometry but captures unusable data unless every action is manually re-set — budget time for that, or pre-build a proper reversed route.
- When you genuinely can't tell whether arriving/departing traffic will conflict, the safe default is to land and let it pass — accept the efficiency cost (a precautionary land can cost ~30 min in charging, transit back to the start point and RTH) as the price of certainty until you can read the situation confidently.

## Human Factors / Reflections
- **Tooling built this period:** a morning-wind predictor keyed to each hub now estimates flights, airborne time, coverage and finish time, and flags on-time-delivery risk against the ~1:15 pm site-report deadline. Early finding — coverage holds across almost all wind bands; the real risk is a *late finish*, and lateness isn't only wind-driven (battery overheat, charge time and heli deconfliction have pushed even light-wind days late). Caveat: per-band samples are still small and sharpen with use; confirm the report timezone (AEST) against the site SLA (AWST) before relying on the flag.
- Schedule pilots and hubs to **actual workload**, not a blanket allocation. Hubs differ substantially (e.g. one finishing in ~150 min vs another needing ~250 min); assigning a flat 3–4 hours to both, then stacking another task afterwards, creates avoidable delivery delays. Match the busier hub to the earlier/most-suited slot to keep everyone on time.
- A 9 am start typically lands the last flight around 12:45 for a 1:15 pm delivery — tight. A 10 am start (forced by covering another site's 9 am flight) all but guarantees a late delivery; reassigning the hub is better than absorbing the delay.
- **Sequence flights to balance upload against charging:** fly the last flight with the *smallest* dataset and the second-last with the *largest*, so both aircraft finish uploading at roughly the same time. If a landing needs a long charge, make the next mission longer to match. In low wind, with confidence the mission will finish, there's no need to push the battery to 100%.
- On dual-dock hubs, don't fly a small area immediately after a big one — when you land, the other dock may not have finished charging or uploading.
- At start-of-day on dual-dock hubs, if the sky isn't yet bright, fly the furthest sub-area first so you can take off earlier — by the time the aircraft reaches the start point the light has improved. (A card of ~1,000–1,100 images can still upload before the next flight.)
- Use image-count/time highlights and verify capture *later* rather than waiting for data to appear on screen — this maximises airtime.
- Housekeeping still bites: docks left un-formatted and a full E-SDK card have caused avoidable problems — check storage state as part of setup.
- Task focus narrows options: forgetting a simple capability (that the aircraft can shoot stills/video directly) and reaching for screenshots instead is a sign of the same tunnelling. Build familiarity so the simple option comes to mind first under pressure.

## Case Studies & Incident Reflections

### Case Study 1: Repeated connection loss in a deep open-pit mapping area
**Scenario:** A mapping mission required descending into a deep open-pit mining area to hold the required ground sample distance (~3 mm GSD), which put the aircraft around 110 m AGL near the pit floor. At that height and depth the control link dropped near the end of the run. The pilot re-flew the *identical* mission hoping the link would hold on the second attempt; it dropped again at the same point, triggering a smart RTH both times.

**Outcome:** On the third attempt the pilot relaxed the GSD requirement to ~3.2 mm (allowing a higher ~120 m AGL), which kept the aircraft above the worst of the pit shielding and completed the run — but only just, on limited remaining battery. Because FH2 cannot resume after a disconnection-triggered smart RTH, each failed attempt meant re-flying from the start, compounding the battery and time cost.

**Lessons:**
- When a mission fails at a repeatable point, **change the plan before retrying** (altitude/GSD or routing) rather than re-running the same mission hoping the link holds.
- Place the start point closest to the known worst connection spot, so a dropout can be reloaded and retried quickly instead of surfacing only at the end of a long run.
- A modest GSD relaxation can be the difference between a completed run and none — know the tolerance you actually have (roughly 2–3× GSD) before you're airborne.
- Build a reversed route for long missions in shielded areas so a late dropout doesn't force a full re-fly.

### Case Study 2: Departing traffic overhead with compressed vertical separation
**Scenario:** Drone mapping was underway near an aerodrome at roughly 1,600–1,700 ft AMSL. A scheduled regional fixed-wing aircraft departed on a track that, per the job card, the drone was kept laterally clear of ("do not fly on the departure path"). The departure was delayed ~5 minutes and was first heard only when the crew made its airborne radio call. On climb-out the aircraft turned and passed close overhead at ~5,000 ft AMSL. A separate occasion saw a similar departure pass overhead at ~6,000 ft, also after a delayed departure.

**Outcome:** Separation was tight but acceptable on both occasions and no loss of separation occurred. The encounters made clear that lateral clearance from the nominal departure path did not guarantee comfortable vertical clearance once the aircraft turned during climb.

**Contributing factors identified afterwards:**
- The job-card basis for separation was a single nominal track, which didn't account for the climb turn bringing traffic overhead.
- Delayed departures meant the first cue was the airborne radio call, leaving little reaction time.
- A high authorised ceiling narrowed the available vertical gap to climbing traffic.

**Lessons:**
- "Stay off the departure path" addresses the nominal track only — plan for the aircraft turning during climb and confirm the *vertical* separation is genuinely sufficient when it passes overhead.
- A quiet frequency is not confirmation that scheduled traffic hasn't launched; delayed departures are normal.
- Treat a high authorised operating ceiling as a risk to manage, not headroom to use by default.
- Where a job card's separation basis is a single track or time buffer, validate it against the aircraft's actual climb/turn profile for the day's runway in use.

### Case Study 3: Precautionary landing under arrival-path uncertainty
**Scenario:** Operating near an aerodrome for the first time without a confident read of the departure/arrival procedures, the pilot could not tell whether inbound traffic would conflict with the drone's position given the day's wind and runway in use. The job-card guidance ("do not fly on the arrival/departure path") did not resolve the ambiguity on its own.

**Outcome:** The pilot landed as a precaution and let the traffic pass. The arriving aircraft in fact used the opposite side and there was no conflict at all — but establishing that cost roughly 30 minutes in charging plus the transit back to the start point and associated RTH: a real operational delay and efficiency loss.

**Lessons:**
- When you can't confidently assess a conflict, landing and letting traffic pass is the correct default — the safety margin is worth the time.
- The cost of that default (charging, transit, RTH) is real, so invest in reading DAPs and runway-in-use logic beforehand to reduce how often uncertainty forces a precautionary land.
- Runway-in-use depends on wind — the same drone position can be clear under one wind and overhead-conflicted under the reciprocal. Assess it fresh each day rather than carrying yesterday's picture.
- Familiarity built in a low-stakes setting turns "land to be safe" into "assess and continue safely," recovering the lost efficiency without lowering the safety bar.
