# BVLOS Operations — Lessons Learnt (1 May – 15 June 2026)

## Pre-Flight Planning & Risk Assessment
- Always confirm flight paths are well clear of No-Fly Zones (NFZs) before takeoff — aircraft may pause mid-flight and refuse to continue if too close, forcing an in-air replan that wastes battery and time.
- Become fully familiar with loading, amending, and switching flight plans *before* operating — in-flight unfamiliarity can turn a minor replan into a mission-ending delay.
- Avoid mixing incompatible capture modes (e.g. different oblique/ortho combinations) within the same mission — this can significantly increase data processing time without improving data quality.

## Airmanship & Flight Technique
- Fly low when facing headwind, higher when tailwind — improves endurance by working with the wind.
- For mapping missions, use at least three flight lines with increased overlap, oriented along the longest edge of the area; adjust the angle as needed.
- For elevation/terrain work, flying toward a wall/slope can improve data quality.
- Plan the mission so the furthest point from home is the start point — as the flight progresses and the aircraft gets closer to home, the system is "less conservative," allowing more time before RTH triggers (this can be the difference between finishing the area in one flight or not).
- For panorama captures, manual flying between waypoints reduces downtime compared to waiting for the aircraft to auto-trigger the next waypoint after photo stitching.
- Balance data upload time against battery charging time — uploads can be paused and resumed across charging cycles, but be mindful of how data capture sessions are split.
- Rough rule of thumb: ~4% battery per km to return to home and land — use this for quick in-flight go/no-go decisions on whether to push further or head back.
- Be aware that strong winds can flip drones on the ground or during takeoff/landing.

## Airspace Coordination & Traffic Deconfliction
- Favour direct engagement and real-time deconfliction discussions with other airspace users over rigid, overly-restrictive predefined flight parameters.
- When working near scheduled aircraft movements, cross-reference wind data (e.g. TAFs) and typical departure/arrival profiles to better predict whether another aircraft will actually intersect your flight path, rather than relying purely on fixed time-based buffers.
- Use multiple situational awareness sources together (radio, ADSB, flight tracking apps) for a more flexible, adaptive picture than a single source alone.
- During automated RTH, gimbal/camera control may be restricted, limiting the ability to visually track nearby traffic while the aircraft recovers itself.
- When transit times between two parties differ significantly (e.g. helicopter vs drone speeds), the margin for safe recovery shrinks rapidly as one party closes in — plan conservative cut-off points well before the margin disappears.

## Communications (CRM)
- Be aware of unit confusion between metric (AGL in metres) and aviation convention (AGL in feet) when communicating altitudes — convert explicitly to avoid ambiguity.
- Two of your own aircraft/crews may be able to hear a third party's transmissions clearly while being unable to hear each other — this can be a coverage, line-of-sight, or configuration issue and is worth investigating, as it affects situational awareness during concurrent operations.
- If simultaneous radio calls cause an unclear transmission to an external party, the crew member with better signal clarity to that party should complete the call.

## In-Flight Decision-Making
- When a mission can't be completed (e.g. 90% done, low battery), weigh the risk of pushing on vs triggering RTH early. Insufficient battery margin for landing should take priority over completing the last few percent.
- After loading a second mission, the new flight path/area may not display correctly on the map even though it is executing correctly — don't assume a mission has failed just because the display doesn't update; monitor actual aircraft behaviour.
- Predicted timing for other traffic (e.g. another aircraft "about to depart") can be wrong — delays of 10–15 minutes are possible. Ending your mission early based on an assumption can cause your own delivery delays without actually being necessary. Where safe, continue monitoring rather than aborting purely on an estimate.

## Human Factors / Reflections
- Early arrival on site isn't automatically "good" the way it is for scheduled transport — turning up too early can mean the activity you're there to capture hasn't started yet.
- Time discipline matters even when there's no formal penalty for being late — onward activities (e.g. other site work) may depend on your operation finishing on schedule.
- Building familiarity with system quirks (e.g. map display not updating, unit conversions, RTH behaviour) before they matter in a live mission reduces stress and error rate.

## Case Studies & Incident Reflections

### Case Study 1: In-flight replan due to NFZ proximity, leading to incomplete mission
**Scenario:** During a mapping mission, the planned flight path passed close to a No-Fly Zone boundary. The aircraft's geofencing/obstacle-avoidance logic paused the mission mid-flight and would not continue along the original path. The pilot, being unfamiliar with the in-flight replanning workflow, spent several minutes hovering while loading and adjusting a new flight plan.

**Outcome:** The replan and associated hover time consumed enough battery that the mission could not be completed — approximately 10% of the planned area remained uncaptured. The pilot then had to decide whether to push on to finish the last 10% (cancelling the automatic Return-To-Home trigger) or accept the incomplete capture and return safely. The decision was made to prioritise a safe return with adequate battery margin over completing the final 10%, accepting the incomplete dataset.

**Lessons:**
- Pre-flight planning should include a buffer check against NFZ boundaries with enough margin that geofence pauses are unlikely, not just technically compliant.
- Pilots should rehearse the in-flight replan/reload workflow in a low-stakes setting before relying on it operationally — minutes lost to an unfamiliar UI directly convert to lost mission completion margin.
- When battery margin and mission completion are in conflict, returning with a safe margin should take priority; an incomplete dataset is recoverable with a follow-up flight, a battery-depleted off-dock landing is not.
- Time pressure from external stakeholders (e.g. other activities scheduled to commence on site once flight operations cease) can add pressure to "push through," which should be explicitly recognised and not allowed to override the battery-margin decision.

### Case Study 2: Concurrent operations near low-level helicopter traffic — radio coverage gap and timing margin
**Scenario:** Two drone crews were conducting BVLOS operations in proximity to a helicopter conducting a low-level (not above 200 ft AGL) linear infrastructure survey along a known corridor. Position reporting was being used by all parties to maintain deconfliction, with an agreed trigger point along the corridor after which drone operations would cease.

During the operation, the helicopter broadcast a position report. Both drone crews attempted to acknowledge simultaneously, resulting in a "blocked"/unclear transmission being received by the helicopter — i.e. the helicopter could hear both drone crews individually, but the two drone crews could not hear each other's transmissions to know the channel was already in use. One crew, having clearer signal to the helicopter, completed the acknowledgement; both aircraft continued safely and later returned to land as planned.

**Contributing factors identified afterwards:**
- A likely radio coverage / line-of-sight / configuration difference between the two drone operating positions meant they could each hear the helicopter but not each other — a latent issue that had not previously been identified because it had not caused a problem until both needed to transmit at the same moment.
- Unit mismatch in altitude reporting conventions (the drone platform's display uses metres AGL, while aviation radio calls use feet AGL) creates a standing risk of misunderstanding distances/heights between drone crews and manned aircraft if not deliberately converted.
- During an automated Return-To-Home, the drone's camera/gimbal cannot be freely used to visually track nearby traffic, reducing the crew's situational awareness exactly when traffic conflict risk may be highest.
- A separate analysis of relative transit speeds showed that once the helicopter passed the agreed trigger point, it could reach the operational area faster than a drone operating at the far extent of its corridor could return and land — meaning the "cease operations at trigger point" rule provided less real recovery margin than intended, particularly for the furthest-out drone.

**Lessons:**
- Radio coverage between cooperating crew positions should be verified as part of setup for concurrent operations, not assumed from the fact that all parties can hear the primary traffic.
- Altitude unit should be clear (e.g. "200 feet AGL, that's approximately 60 metres AGL") when communicating with manned aircraft, to remove ambiguity.
- Deconfliction trigger points based on a single landmark/waypoint should be validated against actual relative speeds and worst-case drone position (furthest from home) to confirm genuine recovery margin, not just notional separation.
- Recommendations arising from this event included reviewing the deconfliction procedure to either widen the time/distance buffer before the trigger point, or restrict drone operations at the corridor extremities while the helicopter is in the relevant phase of its transit.

### Case Study 3: Schedule-driven decision-making — early mission termination vs continuing operations
**Scenario:** A drone mission was commenced earlier than the originally scheduled window, on the assumption that completing early would be beneficial. However, another airspace user (operating on the basis of the originally scheduled timeframe) entered the area while the drone was airborne, having had no expectation that drone operations would already be underway at that time.

**Outcome:** The mission was terminated and the aircraft grounded as a safety precaution. Operations did not resume until the team had conducted a review of the encounter and agreed on updated coordination protocols, after which BVLOS operations were approved to continue.

**Lessons:**

Agreed operating windows exist for a reason — they form part of how other airspace users plan their own activity. Deviating from the agreed timeframe (even with good intentions, such as finishing early) removes the basis on which other users assessed the airspace as clear.
"Earlier is better" is not a safe default for BVLOS scheduling — unlike many other contexts, an early start can create exactly the kind of unplanned encounter that a fixed window is designed to prevent.
Grounding the aircraft and pausing operations until a proper review and protocol update had occurred was the correct response — it allowed the cause to be understood and coordination procedures to be tightened before resuming, rather than continuing under an assumption that the encounter was a one-off.
Any change to a previously agreed operating schedule (start time, end time, or area) should be communicated to all relevant parties in advance, not decided unilaterally based on operational convenience.
