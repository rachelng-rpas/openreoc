# 05 — Best Practice: Beyond CASA Minimums

This section distinguishes between what CASA **requires** and what experienced operators **recommend**. Both matter — but it's important to know which is which.

Last review: 18 April 2026

---

## Regulatory Requirements vs Industry Best Practice

Meeting the minimum doesn't always mean your operation is running as safely or efficiently as it could. Good operators go further, not because CASA told them to, but because it makes their operations safer, more professional, and easier to manage as they grow.

Unlike manned aviation where pilots must meet defined recency, flight review, and proficiency check requirements. RPAS regulations do not mandate an equivalent system at the same level.

In practice, it is still good industry practice to implement internal recurrent training and periodic competency checks. This helps maintain safety standards, ensures consistency across crews, and strengthens compliance during audits.

This section clearly separates the two:

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 🔴 **Required by CASA** | A specific obligation under CASR Part 101, MOS 101, or your Operations Manual. Failure to comply is a regulatory breach. |
| 🟢 **Recommended best practice** | Something that experienced operators do because it improves safety, quality, or efficiency. Not failing to do it is not a regulatory breach — but doing it makes your operation stronger. |

## Continuous Training and Review

### What CASA Requires 🔴

MOS 101 s10.03(2) requires the CRP to keep records of training provided to personnel. MOS 101 s10.08 also requires that non-RP crew are trained to perform their duties safely. Your Ops Manual defines the specific training required before someone can operate under your ReOC.

However, MOS 101 does **not** prescribe:
- Fixed recurrent training intervals (e.g. annual checks)
- Formal competency reassessment cycles
- Structured refresher programs

### What Good Operators Do 🟢

**Regular review of remote pilots**, covering:

- Skills currency — has the pilot flown recently? Are they current on the aircraft types they're authorised for?
- Operational knowledge — do they understand current procedures, airspace requirements, and any recent changes to your operations manual?
- Safety awareness — are they up to date on recent incidents, safety bulletins, and lessons learned?

**Periodic refresher training**, even when not explicitly mandated. This might include:

- Annual knowledge review (theory-based assessment covering regulations, procedures, and emergency response)
- Practical flight assessment (observed flight in normal and emergency scenarios)
- Type currency flights (particularly for aircraft types not flown regularly)
- Scenario-based exercises (e.g. "you lose GPS lock at 80m AGL over a residential area — what do you do?")

**Internal records of ongoing competency**, such as:

- Recurrent training dates and outcomes
- Scenario-based assessment results
- Performance reviews or debrief notes
- Currency checks (recent flight hours by type)

> 🟢 **This is best practice for safety and operational quality, not a strict CASA requirement under MOS 101.** Your Operations Manual may impose its own recurrent training requirements — check your manual and treat those as binding.

### Why It Matters

Even for a two-person operation, recurrent training and review:

- Catches skill decay before it causes an incident
- Provides documented evidence of an ongoing safety culture (which CASA views favourably during audits)
- Helps with insurance — some insurers look for evidence of ongoing training
- Builds confidence in your team and your clients


## Induction Training

### What CASA Requires 🔴

CASA requires that personnel operating under a ReOC are competent for their role. MOS 101 s10.03(2) requires the CRP to record training. Your Operations Manual will define your induction process.

### What Induction Typically Includes 🟢

A well-structured induction program generally covers:

- **Overview of company operations and structure** — what the company does, how it's organised, who is responsible for what.
- **Understanding the Operations Manual** — where to find procedures, how to use the manual in practice.
- **Roles and responsibilities under the ReOC** — what the CRP, RP, observer, and ground handler are each responsible for.
- **Basic flight planning process** — how operations are planned, authorised, and documented in your organisation.
- **JSA and risk assessment expectations** — how to complete a JSA, when a risk assessment is needed, what standard is expected.
- **Safety procedures and emergency response** — what to do if something goes wrong, who to notify, how to respond.
- **Record-keeping responsibilities** — what records the person needs to complete, where to file them, and why it matters.

We might include a seperate project covering how to structure and draft an induction program, combining the insights from a manned aviation background.

> 🟢 **Detailed training structures, competency frameworks, and internal training systems will be covered in a separate module/project focused on personnel qualification and training management.** This guide focuses on record keeping — we include induction here only because induction training generates records that the CRP must maintain.

### How to Record Induction

Induction training is recorded in two places:

1. **Training Log** — one or more rows covering the induction activities (e.g. TR-001: "Initial induction as RP under ReOC — all G1 syllabus items — Pass").
2. **Personnel Register** — the "Induction training completed" and "Induction record form on file" columns updated to Y with the date.

Keep the completed induction record form (whatever form your ops manual specifies) in the person's individual personnel folder.

## Operator Approval Reviews

### What CASA Requires 🔴

CASA requires that personnel are qualified for the specific types of operations they conduct. If your ops manual defines operator-specific approvals (night operations, EVLOS, senior RP, tethering, etc.), the person must hold that approval before conducting those operations.

### What Good Operators Do 🟢

**Track review dates for every approval.** The Personnel Register template includes a "last review date" alongside each approval column. Use this to schedule periodic reviews.

**Set a review cycle.** Even if your ops manual doesn't specify a review frequency, consider reviewing all operator approvals annually. Ask:

- Has the person exercised this approval in the last 12 months?
- Have procedures or regulations changed since their last review?
- Is there any reason to believe their competency has changed?

**Document the review outcome**, even if it's "reviewed, no change, approval continues." A brief note in the Training Log or a review record in the personnel folder provides evidence that you're actively managing competency.

## Building a Safety Culture Through Records

Records aren't just compliance documents, they're tools for improving your operation. Here's how:

### Trend Analysis 🟢

Periodically review your records for patterns:

- Are JSAs identifying the same hazards repeatedly? Should those hazards be addressed at a systemic level?
- Are certain types of operations generating more post-flight notes or incidents?
- Are there patterns in unserviceability reports?
- Is one aircraft generating more maintenance entries than others?

### Lessons Learned 🟢

After any incident, near-miss, or unusual event:

- Record what happened in the operational log.
- Discuss it with your team.
- Consider whether procedures need to change.
- Document the discussion and any changes made.

### Debrief Records 🟢

For complex or high-risk operations, consider recording a brief post-operation debrief:

- What went well?
- What could be improved?
- Were there any unexpected hazards?

## Summary: Required vs Best Practice

| Topic | Required by CASA 🔴 | Recommended Best Practice 🟢 |
|---|---|---|
| Record training given to personnel | Yes — s10.03(2) | — |
| Record non-RP crew training | Yes — s10.08 | — |
| Structured induction program | Depends on your ops manual | Yes — cover key topics listed above |
| Specific recurrent training frequency | Not mandated in MOS 101 (check your ops manual) | Yes — annual review recommended |
| Track approval review dates | Depends on your ops manual | Yes — review all approvals annually |
| Competency reassessment intervals | Not mandated in MOS 101 (check your ops manual) | Yes — periodic flight and knowledge checks |
| Trend analysis of records | No | Yes — review patterns quarterly |
| Post-operation debriefs | No | Yes — especially for complex or high-risk ops |
| Lessons learned documentation | No (unless incident reporting applies) | Yes — improves safety culture |

> This is about using the record-keeping system as a tool for continuous improvement, not just a box-ticking exercise for CASA.
