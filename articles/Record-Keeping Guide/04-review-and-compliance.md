# 05 — Review and Compliance

Keeping records is one thing. Keeping them accurate, complete, and audit-ready is another. This section covers how to review your records, catch problems early, and prepare for CASA scrutiny.

Last review: 18 April 2026

---

## How Often to Review

| Review Type | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| **Per-operation check** | Every operation | Pre-flight columns complete before flight. Post-flight columns complete after flight. Supporting documents (JSA, RA) filed. |
| **Weekly review** | End of each week (if operating regularly) | All operations from the week have complete rows. No blank post-flight sections. Pilot logs updated. |
| **Monthly review** | End of each month | All records for the month are filed and cross-referenced. Training log entries match personnel register updates. Technical logs are current for all aircraft flown. |
| **Quarterly internal audit** | Every 3 months | Full check against the audit checklist (see below). Spot-check random operations for completeness. Review personnel register for accuracy. |
| **Annual review** | Once per year | Full system review. Archive old records. Update templates if needed. Check that all personnel qualifications and approvals are current. Review against current MOS 101 requirements (in case of regulatory changes). |

---

## What to Check: The Quick Checklist

Use this checklist for your per-operation and weekly reviews:

**Operations Record:**
- [ ] Operation ID assigned and sequential (no gaps)
- [ ] All pre-flight columns (B–P) completed before the flight
- [ ] Post-flight columns (Q–T) completed after the flight
- [ ] Document tracking columns (U–W) accurately reflect what's on file
- [ ] JSA actually exists in the job folder (not just marked "Y")
- [ ] Aircraft ID matches the technical log
- [ ] PIC name and ARN match the personnel register
- [ ] If CASA approvals marked "Y," the instrument is on file

**Personnel & Training Record:**
- [ ] Every person who flew or crewed this month appears in the personnel register
- [ ] All training events this month have a row in the training log
- [ ] Training outcomes (pass/fail) have been reflected in the personnel register
- [ ] CRP has signed off on training log entries
- [ ] Assessment forms referenced in the training log actually exist on file

**Aircraft Records:**
- [ ] Technical log updated with flight hours/cycles for each aircraft used
- [ ] Any maintenance or defects recorded
- [ ] Serviceability status is current

**Pilot Logs:**
- [ ] Each RP's pilot log is up to date
- [ ] Hours are consistent with the operational log entries

## Quarterly Internal Audit

Every three months, conduct a more thorough review. This doesn't need to be formal, it's a self-check to catch drift before it becomes a problem.

### Audit Process

1. **Select a sample.** Pick 3–5 random operations from the quarter.

2. **Trace each operation end-to-end.** For each sampled operation, verify:
   - The operation has a complete row in the Operations Record (pre-flight and post-flight).
   - The JSA and any other supporting documents exist in the correct job folder.
   - The PIC listed is in the Personnel Register and was qualified/approved for this type of operation.
   - The aircraft used has a current technical log entry covering that date.
   - The PIC's pilot log includes this flight.

3. **Check personnel currency.** For each active person in the Personnel Register:
   - Are their qualifications still current?
   - Have any operator approvals expired or come due for review?
   - Has any required recurrent training been completed?

4. **Check aircraft currency.** For each aircraft in your fleet:
   - Is the technical log up to date?
   - Are there any outstanding defects?
   - Has scheduled maintenance been completed?

5. **Document findings.** Write a brief note of what you checked and what you found. Even if everything is fine, record that the audit was done. Keep these audit notes in your admin folder.

### What to Do If You Find Problems

| Problem | Action |
|---|---|
| Missing post-flight entry | Complete it now with a note: "Entered retrospectively on [date]" |
| Missing JSA or RA | If the document was completed at the time but not filed, locate and file it. If it was never completed, record the gap and note it as a corrective action |
| Personnel register not updated after training | Update it now. Add a note to the training log cross-referencing the update |
| Pilot log gaps | Have the pilot update their log. Cross-reference against the operational log for accuracy |
| Technical log gaps | Update based on available records. If maintenance was done but not logged, record it with a note explaining the late entry |

The key principle: **fix it, document the fix, and prevent it from happening again.** Don't hide gaps, aviation is around Just Culture.

## Common Mistakes

These are the most frequent record-keeping failures seen in small drone operations:

### 1. "I'll do it later"

Post-flight records completed days or weeks after the operation. By then, details are forgotten or wrong. MOS 101 says "as soon as practicable." That means the same day, ideally immediately after the flight.

**Fix:** Make post-flight record completion part of your pack-up routine. Don't leave the site until the post-flight columns are done.

### 2. "It was the same as last time"

Copying previous entries without checking. Every operation is different — even repeat jobs at the same site. Conditions change, crew changes, aircraft changes.

**Fix:** Complete each row fresh. Use dropdowns and defaults to speed things up, but review each field.

### 3. Marking Documents "On File" When They're Not

Ticking "JSA on file: Y" in the Operations Record but the JSA is sitting on someone's desktop or was never saved properly.

**Fix:** Don't mark a document as filed until you've confirmed it's in the correct folder with the correct name.

### 4. Forgetting to Update the Personnel Register

Training happens, the Training Log gets a new row, but the Personnel Register still shows the old status.

**Fix:** Every time you add a Training Log entry, immediately check and update the corresponding Personnel Register entry.

### 5. Not Keeping Pilot Logs

Many operators treat the operational log as the pilot log. They serve different purposes. The operational log is the organisation's record. The pilot log is the individual's record.

**Fix:** Maintain separate pilot logs. They can be a simple spreadsheet — one per pilot.

### 6. No Technical Log for "Simple" Aircraft

Thinking that a consumer-grade DJI drone doesn't need a technical log. If it's used under a ReOC, it does.

**Fix:** Set up a simple technical log for every aircraft in your fleet, regardless of size or complexity.

### 7. No Backup

All records on one laptop, one USB drive, or one Google account with no backup.

**Fix:** Follow the 3-2-1 backup rule described in the Implementation Guide. Cloud storage with a monthly local backup is the minimum.

### 8. Mixing Personal and Business Records

Flight logs that combine recreational flights, excluded category flights, and ReOC operations without distinction.

**Fix:** Clearly separate ReOC operations from other flying. Use your operation ID prefix (e.g. BH-XXXX) to identify ReOC operations in all records.

## Record Retention and Disposal

When records pass the 7-year retention period:

1. **Verify the retention period has truly expired.** For personnel records, the 7 years starts from when the person **ceased** operating under the ReOC, not from the date of the record.

2. **Don't rush to delete.** There's no harm in keeping records longer than required. Storage is cheap.

3. **If you do dispose of records,** document what was disposed of and when. Keep a simple disposal log.

4. **For digital records,** secure deletion is important if the records contain personal information (names, ARNs, contact details). Consider your obligations under the Privacy Act 1988.

5. **For paper records,** shred rather than bin. Records contain personal information about your personnel and clients.
