## What is a ConOps?

A Concept of Operations (ConOps) is a short standalone document 
(2–5 pages) that tells CASA, in plain language:

- Who you are
- What OONP operations you want to do
- How you intend to do them safely
- Why you've chosen the pathway(s) you've chosen

It is **not** part of your ops manual. It is **not** a per-site plan. 
It describes your overall OONP capability: the types of operations 
you want approval for, across all the locations you might operate.

Think of it as your pitch to CASA: "Here's what we want to do, here's 
how we've thought it through, and here's why we believe we can do it 
safely within the TMI framework."

Everything else you prepare: energy calculations, procedures, consent 
forms, supports what you describe in this document. If your ConOps 
is unclear, CASA won't understand what the rest of your package is for.

## Before You Write — Questions to Work Through

Go through every question below. Write your answers in dot points 
or short paragraphs. Don't worry about formatting yet, this is your 
thinking stage. Once you've answered everything, you have the raw 
material to draft the full document.

### Section 1 — About Your Operation

1. **What is your company name and ReOC number?**

2. **What do you currently do under your ReOC?**  
   (e.g. aerial photography, inspections, surveying, building wash, 
   mapping - list all your current operation types)

3. **Where do you typically operate?**  
   (e.g. Sydney metro, NSW-wide, Australia-wide, specific industries 
   or site types)

4. **What RPA are currently on your ReOC?**  
   (list all, with weight category — you'll narrow down to OONP-specific 
   ones later)

### Section 2 — What OONP Operations Do You Want to Do?

This is the core of your ConOps. Be specific about each operation type.

5. **List every type of operation where you need to fly over or near 
   people.** For each one, describe:
   - What the operation involves (what are you doing and why?)
   - Who will be in the area (crew, clients, public, workers, performers?)
   - Where these operations typically happen (outdoors, indoors, urban, 
     rural, events, worksites?)
   - How often you expect to do this type of operation

   *Example: "Aerial filming at outdoor events such as weddings, 
   graduations, and community festivals. Attendees and audience members 
   will be below and nearby. Operations occur in varied outdoor 
   environments, estimated once or twice per month during peak season."*

6. **For each operation type, which pathway are you using and why?**
   - Can you control the area? → Pathway 1
   - Is the RPA energy below 15J? → Pathway 2
   - Could some operations need both pathways depending on the site? 
     Explain when you'd use each.

   *If you're unsure, review 
   [01 — Overview and Pathways](Step-01-overview-and-pathways.md) first.*

7. **Are there any scenarios where one pathway might not be enough 
   on its own?**  
   (e.g. building wash where the ground area is constrained and you 
   can't fully exclude pedestrians — might you combine P1 for the 
   controlled zone with P2 for the buffer area?)

### Section 3 — Your Fleet

8. **Which specific RPA from your ReOC will you use for OONP?**  
   For each one, note:
   - Model name
   - MTOW (with standard configuration)
   - Does the weight change with different batteries or accessories?
   - Will you use it for Pathway 1, Pathway 2, or both?

9. **Are there any RPA you plan to add to your ReOC for OONP in future?**  
   (e.g. a drone not yet on your certificate — note this as TBC)

10. **For Pathway 2 RPA — what flight modes does it have and what are 
    the max speeds?**  
    (you'll do the full energy calculations later, but you need to know 
    this now to confirm Pathway 2 is viable)

### Section 4 — Crew and Roles

11. **What is the minimum crew for each operation type?**  
    Think about:
    - Remote pilot (always required)
    - Observer
    - Ground support
    - Additional spotters or assistants for larger sites

12. **What does each crew role do during an OONP operation?**  
    Especially the observer: what specifically are they watching for, 
    how do they communicate with the pilot, what actions do they take 
    if someone enters the area?

### Section 5 — How You'll Manage Missions

CASA doesn't review individual missions. They want to know that you 
have a system for managing them internally.

13. **Who decides whether an operation is OONP and which pathway applies?**  
    (usually the CRP)

14. **What tools and checklists will your crew use on each mission?**  
    List what you plan to use. Some of the examples are:
    - Fleet Energy Register (which modes are compliant for which RPA)
    - OONP Job Safety Analysis / risk assessment
    - Consent forms and procedures
    - OONP pre-flight checklist
    - OONP mission report

15. **How will you keep records of OONP operations?**  
    (mission reports, consent forms, training records: what gets 
    stored, for how long)

### Section 6 — Volume and Scope

16. **How often do you expect to conduct OONP operations?**  
    (e.g. once a month, weekly during peak season, as contracted)

17. **Do you want a site-specific or broad approval?**  
    A broad (non-site-specific) approval means you can conduct OONP 
    anywhere in Australia within your approval conditions, and plan 
    individual missions internally. A site-specific approval ties you 
    to named locations. Most operators want broad.

## Drafting Your ConOps

Once you've answered all questions, you have everything you need. 
Your ConOps document can follow this structure:

### Suggested Format

| Section | What to Include | Source Questions |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1. Operator Overview | Company, ReOC details, what you currently do | Q1–4 |
| 2. Purpose of Application | What approval you're seeking, which pathways, broad or site-specific | Q6, Q17 |
| 3. Operation Types | Each operation type described with scenarios, environment, pathway logic | Q5–7 |
| 4. RPA Fleet | Which RPA for OONP, weights, configurations, future additions | Q8–10 |
| 5. Crew Composition | Minimum crew per operation type, roles and responsibilities | Q11–12 |
| 6. Mission Management | Internal decision process, tools, checklists, record keeping | Q13–15 |
| 7. Expected Volume | How often, seasonal patterns, growth expectations | Q16 |

### Length and Tone

- 2–5 pages is fine. Don't pad it.
- Write in plain, professional language. CASA assessors read dozens 
  of these, clear and direct is better than long and detailed.
- Be specific about what you will do, not vague about what you might do.
- If something is TBC (e.g. a wash drone model), say so. CASA would 
  rather see honesty than guesswork.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Too vague** — "We want to fly near people for various commercial 
   purposes" tells CASA nothing. Name your operation types and describe 
   realistic scenarios.

2. **Too site-specific** — Unless you're applying for a site-specific 
   approval, don't describe one particular site. Describe the types 
   of environments you'll operate in.

3. **Forgetting crew roles** — CASA wants to see that you've thought 
   about who does what, especially the observer role.

4. **Not linking operations to pathways** — Every operation type should 
   clearly map to Pathway 1 or Pathway 2 (or both, with explanation of 
   when each applies).

5. **Not mentioning mission management** — CASA needs confidence that 
   you can self-manage missions. Show you have a system.

## Using AI to Help Draft?

Once you've answered the 17 questions above, you can paste your 
answers into an AI assistant and ask it to draft your ConOps in the 
format described. Give it:

- Your answers to all 17 questions
- The suggested format table above
- Any specific language or tone preferences

Review everything the AI produces. It won't know your operations 
as well as you do. Check that the scenarios are realistic, the 
crew compositions make sense for your actual team, and the pathway 
selections are correct for your RPA.
